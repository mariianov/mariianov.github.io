Music and storytelling

For Szymon Slusarz-Kowalczyk, the day when Spotify Wrapped gets released is a holiday of sorts. A junior engineering student at the University of Missouri, he said Wrapped combines his knack for math and statistics with his love for music.

"I also just forget everything I listened to at the beginning of the year, and I like to rediscover things about myself and see how I have changed," Slusarz-Kowalczyk said.

Slusarz-Kowalczyk said he loves music that evokes feelings and tells stories. His favorite song of the year is Ofelia by Kiltro. The band appeals to him because of its "very fun beats and amazing Latin guitar." The song tells the story of unrequited love.

"In the song, they repeat 'It's alright, it's a lie' so much to the point where they sound practically the same. There are even arguments by fans over what the correct lyric is. I love the anguish of it," he said.

AI-assisted music

"Walk My Walk" is not the only AI-generated song that's gaining popularity; nor is 2025 the first time a song was made using modern technology, said Pete Zambito.

Music has always lifted Zambito's spirits and made him feel better, he said. So much so, he decided to pursue it professionally and is now an associate teaching professor and assistant director of athletic bands at the University of Missouri. He specializes in percussion and has toured the Southeast and Midwest.

Zambito said musicians have frequently gotten assistance from whatever technology was available to them. Even the Beatles made a lot of their music with overdubbing, creating tracks they could not replicate fully live. However, the band still needed someone who understood how to engineer sound correctly. This is the main difference between using technology to assist with music creation then and now, Zambito said.

Now, sending a prompt to an AI tool will suffice. The quality of the end product is a different question, Zambito said. In his experience, AI-generated music does not sound good. At the same time, the success of "Walk My Walk" proves otherwise.

Whether AI-generated or AI-assisted music is a threat to human artists is a tricky question, Zambito said.

"Even if you are really good, however you define that, it is still really hard to make it big in the arts," he said.

On the one hand, if an AI song is charting, it means that someone else's original music. On the other hand, with the exception of the world's biggest artists, musicians cannot make a living out of streams, Zambito said.

Instead, what has been growing a lot, especially since Covid-19 restrictions were lifted, is live performances, which is something AI cannot do, yet. Zambito said if artists are willing to put in the time, effort and money to do live shows, they might be able to connect with their audiences better.

Zambito's favorite release of the year is Olivia Dean's album titled The Art of Loving, which was recommended to him by a student.

Billboard's top songs throughout the year

Each week, Billboard releases its Hot 100 charts which ranks songs based on streaming data, radio airplay and sales. The exact methodology has been kept confidential.

Watch the video below to hear how the top song changed throughout 2025.

So, what was the top song?

One way to establish the song of the year is to look at all songs that have ranked first and count up the number of weeks they held this position. However, this approach would be detrimental to songs that were consistently ranked second or third for several months, compared to a 5-week one hit wonder.

To combat this, each week a song can be assigned a score between 1 and 100, where the song that was ranked first gets 100 points, a song that was ranked second gets 99 points and so on all the way to the song that was ranked 100th, which would get it 1 point. These points can then be added up to reveal the song with the highest yearly score.

No, music doesn't make you smarter

But listening to and being involved in music does have a lot of benefits said Wendy Sims, director of undergraduate and graduate studies in music education at the University of Missouri. Sims said music at school helps students engage better with learning, deal with changes, understand cultures and more. Overall, music is a great way to socialize with other people, and it can enhance the quality of life.

Sims said she realized she wanted to teach music when she brought her guitar to a pre-school, while volunteering there as a junior high student, and all the kids loved singing along with her.

Music is all around us, Sims said. People sing at concerts, parties, sporting events, religious holidays and so on. The purpose of music education is to make people more accustomed to music, not "find the next little Mozart," Sims said.

People use music throughout their lives, and we want them to be more comfortable, she said.

Sims said it is important to expose children, and people, to all kinds of music. The reason academic music education focuses more on classical pieces is to make sure children are exposed to all sorts of styles, and not just popular music, which they can find online or on the radio.

Sims's favorite song of the year is Abracadabra by Lady Gaga.

In order to reap the most benefits out of listening to music, Sims recommended that people:

  • Explore different kinds of music,
  • Consider common themes and elements among favorite music,
  • Read about music,
  • Think about what musicians who created a particular piece wanted to convey.
